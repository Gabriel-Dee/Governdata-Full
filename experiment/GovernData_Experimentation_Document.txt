GovernData Platform
Experimental Evaluation Document
Healthcare Compliance Governance · Policy Enforcement & Audit Integrity

Overview & Experimental Context
The experimental evaluation of the GovernData platform is designed to assess how different governance strategies impact the enforcement and auditability of healthcare compliance policies such as those derived from HIPAA and GDPR. Rather than treating governance as a single mechanism, this study evaluates it across two key dimensions: policy enforcement and audit integrity.
The experiments aim to determine how effectively different policy engines — a custom JSON-based policy model and a policy-as-code approach using Open Policy Agent (OPA) — can enforce access control decisions, and how different audit storage mechanisms (centralized database versus blockchain-based logging) affect the reliability and trustworthiness of compliance evidence.
To achieve this, a controlled experimental environment was developed using a simulated EHR system integrated with the governance platform. Identical access scenarios — both authorized and unauthorized — are executed across different configurations of the system. These configurations vary in terms of the policy enforcement mechanism used and the audit storage strategy applied. Additionally, adversarial scenarios such as log tampering are introduced to evaluate the system's ability to detect and preserve evidence of unauthorized modifications.

EXPERIMENT 2  —  DB vs Blockchain Audit Integrity

Objective
Determine whether blockchain-based audit logging provides stronger tamper-evidence guarantees than a traditional centralized database when access control logs are maliciously modified or deleted.
Scenario Description
Step 1
Perform a series of access requests (Allow and Deny decisions)

Step 2
Store audit logs in: (a) Database only  |  (b) Blockchain only

Attack
Manually modify a DB log entry  |  Manually delete a DB log entry

Observe
Can the original decision still be proven? Is the tampering detectable?

Expected Results
Database-only logs will be modified without detection, making it impossible to prove the original decision. Blockchain logs should remain immutable, providing cryptographic proof of the original access decision even after a DB attack.

EXPERIMENT 3  —  Hybrid Audit Storage (DB + Blockchain)

Objective
Validate that a hybrid audit model — storing logs simultaneously in both a centralized database and on the blockchain — provides the best balance between query performance and tamper-evident integrity.
Scenario Description
Setup
All access logs written to both DB and blockchain simultaneously

Attack
Modify the DB record for a specific access event

Observe
Compare DB record vs blockchain record — do they match? Is the discrepancy detected?

Expected Results
After tampering with the DB record, querying the blockchain should reveal the original decision. The mismatch between DB and blockchain records should be automatically or manually detectable, demonstrating that the hybrid model provides both accessibility and integrity.

EXPERIMENT 4  —  Policy Versioning Traceability

Objective
Confirm that the governance platform correctly tracks which version of a policy was active at the time of each access decision, enabling complete audit trail reconstruction even after a policy change.
Scenario Description
Step 1
Execute access request using Policy v1 — record decision

Step 2
Update the active policy to v2 (modify access rules)

Step 3
Execute the same access request again — record decision

Observe
Are decisions traceable to the correct policy version? Is version metadata stored in the audit log?

Expected Results
Each decision in the audit log should be annotated with the exact policy version active at that time. Decisions made under v1 and v2 should be independently verifiable, even if they produce different outcomes for the same request.

Key Insight to Uphold
A hybrid governance architecture combining policy-as-code enforcement with tamper-evident audit mechanisms provides stronger compliance assurance than traditional centralized approaches.

What Blockchain Does and Does NOT Do
✗  Blockchain does NOT...
✓  Blockchain DOES...
Enforce or prevent access decisions in real time
Provide tamper-evident records of past decisions
Replace a policy engine for rule evaluation
Enable trust and accountability after the fact
Improve access decision latency
Preserve cryptographic proof of original logs
Define what is or is not compliant
Support audit trail integrity under adversarial conditions

Limitations to Acknowledge
	•	Small-scale testing environment — results may not scale directly to production hospital systems
	•	Synthetic dataset used — no real patient data or real EHR integration
	•	Limited blockchain deployment — simplified setup, not a full distributed ledger network
	•	Latency measurements taken on a single machine — network latency not modeled
	•	Qualitative assessments (policy expressiveness) are subjective and rater-dependent
